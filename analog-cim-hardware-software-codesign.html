Solving Analog CIM Problems in Hardware and Software

Analog compute-in-memory is not one architecture and accuracy is not one problem. SRAM charge-domain macros, RRAM crossbars, PCM tiles, embedded-flash arrays, and heterogeneous processors fail differently. The strongest papers recover useful accuracy by co-designing devices, circuits, converters, digital units, compilers, training, calibration, runtime maintenance, and validation.

Research verdict

The papers show scoped solutions, not a universal accuracy cure

Analog accuracy is now engineerable for selected models and operating conditions. NeuRRAM, IBM PCM systems, heterogeneous memristor/SRAM processors, and full-stack memristor platforms demonstrate that cross-layer mitigation can approach a declared software baseline. The 2026 reviews still identify variability, nonlinearity, drift, endurance, converters, parasitics, and mitigation overhead as active deployment barriers. A valid claim must name the workload, baseline, hardware boundary, calibration state, time horizon, chip population, and cost of every correction.

Hardware aloneNecessary, not sufficientBetter cells and ADCs cannot choose model partitioning or create noise robustness.
Training aloneNecessary for weak regimesNoise injection cannot repair saturation, catastrophic faults, missing dynamic range, or a broken ABI.
Compiler aloneAllocates the error budgetMapping and fallback work only when the target exposes measured capabilities and calibration state.
Runtime alonePreserves the deployed stateCalibration and refresh cannot recover a model that was never trained or mapped for the hardware.

For a 4-bit LLM, the 4-bit boundary normally belongs to weight storage, not to every arrow. A practical hybrid path is W4-like stored weights, 8-bit DAC inputs and ADC outputs, followed by higher-precision digital accumulation. The Analog Foundation Models study uses static 8-bit analog input/output boundaries, keeps attention and other digital work in FP16, and keeps the KV cache in FP16. Its result is evidence for hardware-aware adaptation, not a reason to build a 4-bit ADC and 4-bit accumulator.

Critical-path rule

Optimize in this order: prevent saturation and illegal mappings, meet task accuracy, remove converter and movement stalls, improve utilization, then reduce calibration and lifecycle cost. Optimizing the crossbar MAC first can improve a number that is not limiting the product.

Architecture taxonomy

"NPU on RAM" hides several fundamentally different machines

The memory device determines how a weight is stored. The compute domain determines how products are formed and accumulated. Those choices set the failure mechanisms that software must model.

Digital CIM is a separate escape route. It can provide exact low-bit arithmetic inside SRAM, RRAM, or MRAM, avoiding analog error at the cost of more bit-level operations, addition circuitry, and often lower density or energy advantage. The 2025 mixed processor uses this principle: route sensitive work to lossless SRAM-CIM or tiny digital units instead of demanding that memristor CIM become exact.

Primary-paper solution ledger

What each architecture changed, and what remained outside the claim

Filter by memory or contribution. "Measured" does not always mean a complete end-to-end product; the boundary is stated on each card.

Measured SRAM macro

22-nm charge-domain SRAM CIM

Problem: multibit analog SRAM normally loses precision through mismatch, parasitics, noise, and converter cost.

Hardware: a passive C-2C charge ladder, 9T SRAM, local weight multiplexing, and an 8-bit MAC design target linearity across PVT.

Software: circuit and system analysis selects precision and validates the analog error budget; weights remain ordinary digital SRAM bits.

Evidence: 32.2 TOPS/W peak, less than 0.5% MVM error, and less than 1% measured error deviation across PVT.

Boundary: macro evidence, not a full model-to-runtime system; SRAM remains volatile and area intensive.

Fully measured datasets

NeuRRAM RRAM chip

Problem: wire/driver drop, programming resolution, relaxation, coupling, and limited ADC range made software-only simulation optimistic.

Hardware: 48 reconfigurable cores, transposable dataflow, differential signed weights, and voltage-mode neuron sensing improve utilization and dynamic range.

Software: model-driven calibration, measured-noise training, continuous analog programming, and progressive chip-in-loop fine-tuning.

Evidence: complete hardware runs reached 99.0% MNIST, 85.7% CIFAR-10, and 84.7% Google speech-command accuracy.

Boundary: FPGA supplied intermediate buffers and partial sums; peak array efficiency excludes intermediate transfer.

Measured programming method

Gradient descent-based PCM programming

Problem: cell-wise write-verify depends on precise low-current reads and can miss array nonlinearities that determine the actual MVM result.

Hardware: two 14-nm PCM cores, each with a 256 x 256 array, execute random-probe MVMs during deployment calibration.

Software: gradient descent minimizes measured MVM error and adjusts programming pulses, even when the best physical conductances differ from nominal weight targets.

Evidence: a scaled ResNet-9 hardware run improved from 84.75% to 86.01% CIFAR-10 accuracy; the programming-error advantage persisted for 24 hours.

Boundary: digital gradient calculation and non-MVM work remained off-array; repeated writes cost time and endurance, and each method received one complete inference run.

Integrated mixed-signal chip

64-core PCM inference chip

Problem: an analog tile alone cannot execute activations, communication, convolution support, or complete LSTM layers.

Hardware: 64 PCM AIMC cores, an on-chip communication network, and digital activation/processing units form a complete mixed-signal data path.

Software: mapping and precision modes select one-phase high-throughput or four-phase high-precision MVM execution.

Evidence: near-software accuracy on ResNet and LSTM, with 2.48 TOPS/W high-precision or 9.76 TOPS/W low-precision modes.

Boundary: accuracy and efficiency still depend on model mapping, device programming, and the selected precision mode.

End-to-end KWS; hybrid RNNT

PCM speech recognition and transcription chip

Problem: large speech models amplify programming error, offset, saturation, and cross-tile movement; NVM writes are slow and finite-endurance.

Hardware: 34-tile chips, four PCM devices per cell, an AB differential method, bias rows, pulse-duration routing, and five chips for RNNT.

Software: HWA training and input pruning for KWS; calibration and per-column rescaling; weight expansion for the 45-million-weight RNNT.

Evidence: KWS measured 86.14% versus 86.75%; RNNT measured 9.258% WER versus 7.452% FP baseline.

Boundary: RNNT intermediate data and auxiliary computation used an x86 host; the chip lacked product digital cores and SRAM.

Measured heterogeneous processor

Memristor, SRAM, and digital mixed CIM

Problem: memristor CIM is dense but variation sensitive; lossless SRAM CIM is accurate but large and volatile.

Hardware: memristor-CIM, SRAM-CIM, tiny digital units, and INT/FP modes coexist on one edge processor.

Software: layer- and kernel-granular sensitivity analysis partitions work by error tolerance and format requirements.

Evidence: less than 0.45% degradation with 40.91 TFLOPS/W on ResNet-20/CIFAR-100 and 28.63 TFLOPS/W on MobileNet-v2/ImageNet.

Boundary: accuracy is preserved by heterogeneous routing, not by eliminating analog variation.

Transformer weight layers in hardware

ALBERT on a 14-nm PCM chip

Problem: transformer projections have task-dependent sensitivity, shared weights see different activation distributions, and PCM drifts with time.

Hardware: 35 million PCM devices, 34 tiles, multi-device weight encoding, and a 2D mesh store all 7.1 million unique ALBERT weights.

Software: task-specific HWA noise fine-tuning, mapping, per-use column scale/offset calibration, and periodic recalibration.

Evidence: HWA improved average measured GLUE accuracy by 4.4%; recalibration reduced long-term drift loss.

Boundary: attention, GELU, softmax, layer normalization, pooling, and classification ran in software; some tasks used representative subsets.

Measured multi-chip full stack

OpenCIMTC full-stack memristor system

Problem: fixed hardware dataflow, manual placement, and manual parameter tuning prevent diverse models from deploying reliably.

Hardware: eight flexible memristor chips plus FPGA/CPU control support signed inputs/weights, multiple mappings, and model-dependent dataflows.

Software: CIM IR, compiler, simulator, optimizer, automatic placement, post-deployment training, and inference-time parameter search.

Evidence: six models across four tasks ran automatically; optimizations improved inference accuracy by 3.32% to 9.45%.

Boundary: a research platform with FPGA control, but unusually strong evidence for an actual compiler-optimizer-hardware loop.

Measured converter devices; system modeling

Adaptive memristor ADC

Problem: uniform low-bit ADCs waste codes on layer-specific output distributions; high-bit ADCs dominate area and energy.

Hardware: analog-CAM quantization cells implement programmable overlapping thresholds, one-step conversion, recalibration, and paired-ADC super-resolution.

Software: layer statistics and a constrained Lloyd-Max optimization choose thresholds; program-and-verify maps them to conductance.

Evidence: fabricated 8x8 devices support the model; 5-bit VGG8 reached 89.55% under measured variation and system studies report lower ADC overhead.

Boundary: not a complete fabricated CIM accelerator; Q-cell count grows exponentially at high resolution.

Measured matrix and wireless prototypes

Fault-free analogue computing

Problem: stuck-on/off cells create structured errors that redundancy or retraining may not remove efficiently.

Hardware: two cascaded programmable submatrices and optional compensation layers implement an indirect target matrix and eliminate differential pairs.

Software: gradient-based hardware-aware matrix decomposition uses the measured defect map and hardware constraints.

Evidence: over 99.999% DFT cosine similarity at 39% device faults and a 56-fold wireless bit-error-rate reduction.

Boundary: solves static representation faults for tested matrices; random read noise, ADC error, drift, nonlinear arrays, and complete DNN accuracy remain.

Large-model simulation study

Hardware-aware training across model families

Problem: approximate MVM includes nonlinear and nondeterministic errors that ordinary FP or quantized training never sees.

Hardware abstraction: a realistic crossbar model includes weight, input, output, converter, and circuit nonidealities.

Software: retraining injects the target error model and searches robust operating points for CNNs, recurrent networks, and transformers.

Evidence: many evaluated models recovered floating-point iso-accuracy in simulation; input/output noise was often more damaging than weight noise.

Boundary: simulation evidence depends on model fidelity and does not replace silicon population and lifetime testing.

June 2026 preprint and modeled GPT-2 study

Unified heterogeneous AIMC mapping workflow

Problem: analog/digital assignment is combinatorial, existing methods use incompatible assumptions, and coarse first/last-layer rules do not capture decoder-transformer sensitivity.

Hardware abstraction: PCM-calibrated noise, 512 x 512 tiling, roughly 8-bit converters, digital compute units, memory, interconnect, and maintenance costs.

Software: characterize hardware, profile projection sensitivity, optimize architecture-aware mapping, then validate the complete system.

Evidence: four of 49 GPT-2-small projections dominated marginal sensitivity; 40 had modeled perplexity increase below 3 and became analog candidates.

Boundary: only workflow stages 1-2 were executed; no joint mapping, measured chip, runtime drift response, or end-to-end energy and latency result was produced.

Deep read 1 | measured IBM PCM hardware

Program the measured function, not each cell in isolation

This IEDM paper is the stronger of the two additions because it evaluates the method on fabricated 14-nm PCM cores. Its central lesson belongs in deployment firmware and the compiler/runtime contract.

New idea: conventional write-verify tries to make every read conductance equal its nominal target. Gradient descent-based programming instead minimizes the error of the complete on-chip MVM. It can intentionally leave individual readout weights farther from their nominal values when that compensates peripheral nonlinearity and improves the function the application actually consumes.

1. InitializeRoughly program the target matrix with a short iterative sequence or one-shot pulses.
2. ProbeGenerate synthetic random vectors and execute a batch of MVMs on the physical array; no application dataset is required.
3. Measure lossCompare measured outputs with the ideal target-matrix outputs and minimize normalized MVM error rather than individual-cell error.
4. Update pulsesCompute the gradient digitally, convert it into bounded SET/RESET pulse changes, program selected cells, and repeat.

What the result establishes

  • Accurate single-device reads are not required when the array can execute MVM probes during programming.
  • Function-level optimization can absorb small static nonlinearities that cell-wise conductance matching ignores.
  • Random inputs generalized across tested input distributions and sparsity levels.
  • Batch sizes of at least 64 beat the baseline; 256 or more performed best in the reported sweep.

What remains unresolved

  • Digital gradient computation scales with probe batch, rows, and columns and adds deployment time.
  • Repeated programming pulses consume energy and endurance; the paper does not provide a product lifetime budget.
  • The experiment does not remove random read noise, ADC energy, saturation, PVT variation, or unsupported digital operators.
  • One network and one complete inference run per programming method are not population-level qualification.

Deep read 2 | June 2026 architecture preprint

Profile transformer projections before searching the analog/digital mapping

This paper is current and directly relevant to the software stack, but its GPT-2 result is modeled rather than measured. The useful contribution is a disciplined workflow and a finer mapping granularity.

New idea: a decoder transformer should not be mapped by a blanket first/last-layer rule or by assigning an entire block to one engine. Sensitivity can differ sharply among Q/K/V, attention-output, and FFN projections in the same block. Projection-level profiling first reduces the search space, after which a hardware-specific optimizer can choose the actual analog/digital assignment.

1. CharacterizeHardwareTiles, ADC/DAC, noise, digital units, memory, interconnect, drift

3. MapArchitecture-aware searchAssign analog/digital paths and produce an accuracy-energy-latency Pareto set
4. ValidateComplete systemMeasure task accuracy, analog coverage, latency, energy, and search cost

What is genuinely useful

  • The four-stage workflow prevents sensitivity profiling from being confused with final system mapping.
  • GPT-2 sensitivity was concentrated, and the largest outlier was an internal block-0 attention-output projection.
  • Projection granularity aligns with transformer datapaths while avoiding weight-level routing complexity.
  • The profile can seed heuristic, analytical, or trainable Stage 3 mapping algorithms.

What must happen next

  • Run Stage 3 jointly: one-at-a-time perturbation does not capture interacting errors from many analog projections.
  • Run Stage 4 on a concrete accelerator and count converters, KV cache, memory, communication, latency, and energy.
  • Validate the ranking on silicon, across PCM, ReRAM, and SRAM models, and after time-dependent drift.
  • Repeat on larger decoder models; GPT-2-small does not prove that the same four projections dominate at scale.

Software solution stack

Make measured analog behavior a first-class compilation input

A normal NPU stack assumes deterministic instructions. An Analog CIM stack also consumes a target profile, nonideality model, per-chip state, and declared numerical tolerance.

Training and model preparation

  • Start from a quantized digital baseline so analog loss is not confused with quantization loss.
  • Inject measured weight, input, output, ADC, drift, and PVT distributions at the correct graph locations.
  • Search the noise scale instead of assuming more noise always improves robustness.
  • Use per-channel scaling, clipping, outlier splitting, distillation, and sensitivity analysis.
  • Reserve chip-in-loop tuning for residual behavior the simulator cannot model accurately.

Compiler and optimizer

  • Legalize only operators whose numeric and physical contracts the target can satisfy.
  • Partition sensitive layers or kernels to SRAM/digital units and account for conversion overhead.
  • Lower logical tensors into signed cells, bit slices, physical arrays, converter modes, routes, and partial sums.
  • Use defect-, IR-drop-, saturation-, endurance-, and calibration-aware placement and scheduling.
  • Emit a reproducible report explaining every fallback and predicted error contribution.

Runtime, firmware, and driver

  • Discover device revision, array health, fault map, calibration age, firmware ABI, and precision modes.
  • Program weights transactionally, then select cell-wise write-verify or random-probe MVM optimization from the target capability and deployment budget.
  • Record programming loss, iteration count, pulse count, endurance use, and the final measured operator state.
  • Store per-chip scale, offset, fault, temperature, age, and refresh data separately from portable model content.
  • Monitor saturation, read variance, temperature, drift, timeouts, and device loss during execution.
  • Support recalibration, selective refresh, remapping, digital fallback, rollback, quarantine, and replacement devices.

Simulator and validation

  • Maintain FP32, digital-quantized, ideal-array, nonideality, cycle-model, RTL, and silicon references.
  • Use measured distributions and preserve correlation IDs, model/artifact hashes, calibration version, and device state.
  • Report first divergent layer, error distributions, tails, saturation, and task accuracy instead of one average MVM error.
  • Correlate latency, energy, utilization, converter activity, movement, programming, calibration, and fallback.
  • Qualify across chips, process, voltage, temperature, aging, faults, workloads, and time.

The June 2026 Nature Electronics result

"Fault-free" changes representation, not the laws of analog hardware

What the paper convincingly solves

  • A target matrix can be factored into two adjustable matrices that route around severe stuck-device maps.
  • The representation can eliminate conventional differential pairs and improve density for tested matrices.
  • Gradient-based optimization can incorporate circuit/device constraints before programming.
  • Measured DFT and wireless demonstrations show that the static-fault technique is real.

What remains unsolved

  • Random read noise, ADC/DAC error, input-dependent nonlinearity, IR drop, coupling, temperature, and drift.
  • End-to-end DNN accuracy, unsupported operators, model partitioning, scheduling, runtime, and field maintenance.
  • The cost of the second MVM, analog bridge, compensation layers, calibration, and intermediate dynamic range for every workload.
  • Population-level yield, long-term retention, endurance, and production qualification.

Compiler implication: treat matrix factorization as an optional physical-lowering strategy. It consumes a measured fault map and emits two array programs plus bridge/compensation configuration. The cost model must compare it with spares, remapping, differential encoding, retraining, digital fallback, and replacing the device. The method is valuable precisely because its claim is narrower than "analog is exact."

The adaptive ADC paper contains measured memristor devices and converter characterization, then model and system evaluation under measured variation.
